Resumen

Our subjective world starts every day with the arising, from a sleep state, of consciousness and the sudden perception of ourselves and our environment. The mechanism through this process takes place remains almost unexplored in humans, partly because of the methodological challenges implied in its study. Here we show that waking up is a two-stage process, that involves cortical and subcortical structures, at a very different and precise temporal order. During the first stage, subcortical and sensorimotor structures precede in time and drive most cortical regions, eliciting a very fast, ignition-like activation of the whole brain. After that, a more prolonged second stage takes place, showing the reversed brain pattern of cortical regions driving subcortical structures and cerebellum. This dialog between cortical and subcortical structures shows that the process of waking up has a rich dynamics with potential clinical implications and sheds light into theories of consciousness regarding the role of cortical and subcortical structures that initiate and support conscious states.
